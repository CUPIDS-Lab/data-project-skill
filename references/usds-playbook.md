# USDS Digital Services Playbook

The U.S. Digital Service's 13 plays for delivering public-interest digital services that actually work for the people who use them. Source: https://playbook.usds.gov/

## In one paragraph

The Playbook is a short, opinionated set of practices for public-interest delivery: start from real user needs, design the whole experience simply and accessibly, build iteratively with small teams shipping a minimum viable product fast, decide with data you publish, and default to open. For a data project it mainly shapes the project-design framing, the accessibility bar, and the openness defaults.

## Key practices (sampleable units)

- **Understand what people need** [accessible, inclusive] — research real users early and throughout; write prioritized user stories; test with real people.
- **Address the whole experience** [accessible] — map the online and offline touchpoints, not just the screen.
- **Make it simple and intuitive** [accessible] — plain language, consistent design, and accessibility best practices (this is the root of the accessibility checklist).
- **Build with agile, iterative practices** [reproducible, collaborative] — ship a minimal viable product quickly, release often, keep a backlog, use source control and code review.
- **Use data to drive decisions** [transparent] — monitor the service, track metrics, and publish them internally and externally.
- **Default to open** [transparent, accessible] — publish source code and data, offer bulk downloads and versioned APIs, place data in the public domain or under open licenses, and give users a way to report problems.

## When most relevant

User-facing or civic-facing work, iterative delivery, and openness goals. Strong signal on "users," "service," "iterate," "MVP," "default to open," "publish." Informs L0 framing through L5 publication.

## Caveats / conflicts

Written for government digital-service delivery, so adapt the cadence to research timelines (the principles transfer; "multiple releases per month" may not). It complements rather than replaces the reproducibility and data-handling sources.
